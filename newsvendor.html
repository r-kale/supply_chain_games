📰 The Newsvendor Game

You run a bakery stand. Every morning you decide how many croissants to bake before you know the day's demand. Whatever doesn't sell is thrown away at closing time. You'll run the stand under two different pricing situations — maximize total profit across both.

What you know

  • Daily demand averages 100 croissants and varies day to day — the histogram on the play screen shows how much (a standard deviation of about 30).
  • Demand is the same in both scenarios. Only the prices change.
  • You'll play 10 days in each scenario.

Debrief: too much vs. too little

The right order quantity has almost nothing to do with average demand — it's set by the cost of ordering too much versus the cost of ordering too little.

The newsvendor logic (worth memorizing)

Every extra unit you stock is a bet. It wins Cu (the profit you'd lose by understocking) and risks Co (the cost of overstocking). Stock up to the point where the odds stop being worth it:

Order Q* such that  P(demand ≤ Q*) = Cu / (Cu + Co)  ← the "critical ratio"

  • High margin (scenario 1): losing a sale hurts more than tossing a croissant → critical ratio 0.75 → order above average demand (~120).
  • Low margin (scenario 2): leftovers hurt more than lost sales → critical ratio 0.25 → order below average demand (~80).

The bias to watch for: pull-to-center

Across hundreds of lab studies, people order too little in high-margin settings and too much in low-margin settings — orders get pulled toward average demand (Schweitzer & Cachon, 2000). Check your own averages above. In real supply chains this shows up as chronic understocking of profitable items and overstocking of marginal ones.

Where this applies

Anything perishable or single-season: fashion, produce, holiday stock, spare capacity, hotel rooms, vaccine orders. "Service level" targets in inventory systems are exactly this critical ratio in disguise.

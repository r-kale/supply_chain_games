Flow & Variability

Every supply chain — a factory, a warehouse, a hospital, a coffee shop — is a system of flows. Before we can manage inventory, networks, or partners, we need the three numbers that describe any flow, and the two forces that quietly wreck it.

After this lesson you can

  • Define throughput, work-in-process (WIP), and lead time, and connect them with Little's Law
  • Explain why a perfectly balanced process still underdelivers its average capacity
  • Name the three kinds of buffer and what each one costs
  • Explain why running at ~100% utilization destroys lead times

1. The process view: three numbers

Strip away the detail and any operation is stations connected by flows. Three quantities describe it:

  • Throughput (TH) — units delivered per period. This is what earns revenue.
  • Work-in-process (WIP) — units inside the system, started but not finished. This is tied-up cash.
  • Lead time (LT) — how long one unit takes to get through. This is what customers feel.

They are not independent. Over the long run, every stable process obeys Little's Law:

WIP = Throughput × Lead time

Worked example. A distribution center ships 500 orders/day and orders spend 3 days inside on average. Little's Law says 500 × 3 = 1,500 orders are sitting in the building right now. Want 1-day lead time at the same throughput? WIP must fall to 500. You cannot shorten lead time without either raising throughput or draining WIP — the law permits no exceptions. This is why "reduce inventory" and "respond faster" are the same project.

2. The balanced-line myth

Intuition says: five stations in a row, each averaging 3.5 units/round, should flow 3.5 units/round. Intuition is wrong, for two compounding reasons (Goldratt's famous pairing):

  • Statistical fluctuations. No station does its average every round — it swings above and below.
  • Dependent events. A station can only process what upstream has already delivered. The stations are chained together.

Here's the asymmetry that does the damage: when a station rolls low, everyone downstream starves — that output is lost. When it rolls high, it usually can't use the surplus, because upstream hasn't fed it enough. Deficits propagate; surpluses evaporate. The line's real throughput is meaningfully below the average of its parts, and WIP silently piles up in front of whoever just had a bad round.

This is not a factory curiosity. It is why a 4-airline connection goes wrong so often, why software projects with ten "on-schedule" teams ship late, and why your supply chain underdelivers even when every partner hits their average.

3. Buffers: the price of variability

You cannot wish variability away, so processes protect themselves with buffers. There are exactly three, and every operation uses some mix — the only question is whether by design or by accident:

  • Inventory — stock between stations absorbs swings, but costs cash, space, and obsolescence.
  • Time — a longer promised lead time absorbs swings, but customers hate waiting.
  • Capacity — slack machines and people absorb swings, but idle capacity is expensive.

The superior move — the statistical heart of lean and Six Sigma — is to need less buffering by reducing the variability itself: steadier schedules, standard work, reliable equipment, smaller batches. In the game you're about to play, you'll compare these options head-to-head and see that cutting variability recovers nearly all the lost throughput for free, while buffers and capacity each send you a bill.

4. The utilization trap

One more consequence of variability, worth its own scar-tissue: queueing theory shows that waiting time explodes as utilization approaches 100% — roughly in proportion to variability × utilization / (1 − utilization). At 50% utilization a variability hiccup clears quickly; at 95% it queues for ages. A resource with zero slack has zero ability to absorb a bad day. If your plan needs every station busy every hour to work, your plan has already failed — you just haven't seen it yet.

Now play — 10 minutes

🎲 The Dice Game

Before rolling: write down your prediction for 20 rounds of a balanced 5-station line averaging 3.5/round. Then play, and run all three countermeasure experiments at the end. Watch two things: where WIP piles up, and which countermeasure buys the most throughput per dollar.

After the game — discuss or reflect

  • Your prediction vs. reality: where did the missing units go? (Find them on the board.)
  • Rank the three countermeasures by throughput gained. Now rank them by what they'd cost in a real operation. Which wins?
  • Where is the "dice game" in your workplace — a chain of dependent steps where everyone hits their average but the whole underdelivers?
  • Little's Law check: with bigger buffers, throughput rose and WIP rose. What happened to lead time?

Self-check

1. A support team resolves 40 tickets/day and has 200 tickets open on average. What's the average resolution lead time?

Little's Law: LT = WIP / TH = 200 / 40 = 5 days. Team size, effort, and everything else are already baked into the throughput number.

2. A balanced line of identical stations underdelivers its average capacity because…

Fluctuations + dependency: deficits propagate downstream, surpluses evaporate. The losses don't cancel out — they accumulate.

3. Which intervention improves a variable line's throughput without adding inventory or capacity cost?

Variability reduction recovers the lost throughput for free — that's why lean obsesses over steady takt, standard work, and reliable equipment. Buffers and capacity also work, but you pay for them; 100% utilization makes things dramatically worse.

Key takeaways

  • WIP = Throughput × Lead time. Inventory reduction and speed are the same project.
  • In connected processes, variability is a tax: deficits propagate, surpluses evaporate.
  • Buffer with inventory, time, or capacity — deliberately — or reduce variability and need less of all three.
  • Slack is not waste. A system with no slack cannot absorb a bad day.

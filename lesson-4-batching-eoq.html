Batching & Cycle Stock

Lesson 3 attacked safety stock. Now the other half of the pile: cycle stock exists because we order in batches, and we batch because every order carries a fixed cost. The oldest formula in operations tells you the right batch — and a deeper reading of it tells you why lean factories stopped asking that question.

After this lesson you can

  • Explain the ordering-cost vs. holding-cost trade-off and compute the EOQ
  • Use the cost curve's flatness: when precision in Q matters (rarely) and when it doesn't
  • Explain why reducing the fixed cost K beats optimizing Q — the EOQ logic behind lean/SMED
  • Connect batching to the bullwhip effect you'll create in Lesson 5

1. Why batch at all?

Demand may flow one unit at a time, but supply rarely does. Each order or production run carries a fixed cost K — freight minimums, receiving labor, paperwork, or a machine changeover — that doesn't care how big the batch is. Big batches spread K thin but leave stock sitting; the average inventory from ordering Q at a time is Q/2, each unit costing h per period to hold. Hence the tension:

Cost per period(Q) = K·D/Q  (ordering)  +  h·Q/2  (holding)

Minimizing gives Harris's 1913 Economic Order Quantity — the point where the two costs exactly balance:

EOQ = √(2KD/h)

Worked example (the game). D = 100/week, K = $400, h = $0.30/unit/week: EOQ = √(2×400×100/0.30) ≈ 516 units, about one order every 5 weeks, total cost √(2KDh) ≈ $155/week, split exactly 50/50 between ordering and holding.

2. The curve is flat — read that correctly

The total-cost curve around the EOQ is remarkably forgiving: order 20% off the optimum and costs rise ~2%; order 40% off and it's still under 6%. Two practical consequences:

  • Don't chase decimal places in Q. Round to pallets, truckloads, supplier case packs. The model forgives you.
  • Do chase the inputs. The formula is only as honest as K and h. Holding cost h is chronically understated — capital, shrinkage, obsolescence, and the space that constrains everything else. An honest h is usually 20–35% of item value per year, and it shrinks the "optimal" batch.

3. The lean insight: attack K, not Q

Look at the formula again. Q* moves with √K. That means the powerful lever isn't choosing Q better — it's making K smaller: cut the fixed cost per order by 4× and the optimal batch halves; inventory, lead time (Little's Law, Lesson 1), and staleness all halve with it.

That is precisely what Toyota did. Faced with die changeovers measured in hours, they didn't compute a better batch size — they attacked the changeover itself (SMED: single-minute exchange of die), driving K toward zero until batches of one made economic sense. "Optimal" is a function of constraints you can change. The same move works in the office: e-invoicing shrinks the K of ordering; automated receiving shrinks it again; every shrink justifies smaller, more frequent, smoother flows.

4. Batching's dark side (a preview of Lesson 5)

Batching doesn't just cost holding dollars — it distorts the demand signal. A retailer selling 100/week but ordering 516 every 5 weeks shows its supplier a demand stream of 0, 0, 0, 0, 516. The supplier sees lumps, not the smooth truth, and plans against the lumps. Multiply that up a chain of four tiers and you get the bullwhip — the monster you'll create personally in the next lesson. Small batches aren't just cheap; they're honest.

After the game — discuss or reflect

  • How many attempts did you need? What search strategy would find any EOQ in 4 guesses?
  • The curve is flat near the bottom — so why do so many firms still fight over exact order quantities while ignoring their order-processing costs?
  • List three components of your organization's real K. Which could be cut 10× with today's tools?
  • Where does batching in your world distort a demand signal someone else plans against?

Self-check

1. Demand quadruples (4×) while K and h stay fixed. The optimal batch size…

EOQ = √(2KD/h) — everything under a square root. 4× demand → √4 = 2× batch size. Cycle stock grows slower than the business: a quiet economy of scale.

2. Your EOQ says 500, but the supplier ships pallets of 600. Ordering 600 instead costs roughly…

The cost curve is flat near its bottom: ±20% on Q costs ~2%. Round to the practical container and spend your energy on K and h instead.

3. SMED (cutting changeover time from hours to minutes) affects the EOQ equation by…

Changeover cost IS the K of a production batch. Drive K down and small batches become optimal — this is the EOQ math hiding inside lean's "one-piece flow."

Key takeaways

  • Cycle stock exists because of fixed costs: EOQ = √(2KD/h) balances ordering against holding.
  • The cost curve is flat — round Q to practical sizes and audit K and h instead.
  • The strategic lever is shrinking K (SMED, e-ordering): smaller honest batches, less inventory, faster flow.
  • Batches distort demand signals — the fuel for Lesson 5's bullwhip.

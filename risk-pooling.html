🏭 The Warehouse Consolidation Challenge

You supply four regions — North, South, East, West. Each region's weekly demand averages 100 units with a standard deviation of 30, independent of the others. Your job: find the least inventory that still fills at least 95% of demand. First with four regional warehouses, then with one central warehouse. Unmet demand is lost.

Why pooling works

A warehouse holds two kinds of stock: cycle stock to cover average demand, and safety stock to cover the swings. Averages add up no matter what — but swings partially cancel when you pool them. One region's bad week is another's slow week.

σpooled = σ·√n  (independent demands)  ⇒  safety stock shrinks by √n

Four warehouses → pooling cuts safety stock roughly in half (√4 = 2). Same service, same customers, half the buffer.

When pooling doesn't work

  • Correlated demand. If all regions boom and bust together (ρ → 1), nothing cancels. Try the explorer below.
  • Response time. One central warehouse may be farther from customers — pooling trades inventory for transport time. (Amazon started centralized, then re-decentralized for speed.)
  • Same math powers postponement (pool by delaying differentiation), component commonality, and virtual pooling (share inventory data across stores).

🎲 The Dice Game

A production line with five stations. Each round, every station rolls a die — that's how many units it can pass to the next station (if it has that many waiting). Station 1 never runs out of raw material. Between stations, 4 units start in the buffer.

Think first

Every station averages 3.5 units per round of capacity. The line is perfectly balanced — no station is slower than any other. You'll run 20 rounds.

How many units do you predict the line will deliver? (Naive math says 3.5 × 20 = 70.)

Results

Why a "balanced" line can't hit its average

Two forces gang up on you (Goldratt, The Goal):

  • Statistical fluctuations — every station swings between 1 and 6.
  • Dependent events — a station can only process what upstream has already delivered. A bad roll starves the next station; a good roll can't be "banked" unless there's buffer to absorb it.

The losses don't average out — deficits propagate, surpluses evaporate. That's why real throughput lands below 3.5/round, and why work-in-process piles up in front of whoever just rolled badly.

What would help most? Try the countermeasures

Each experiment simulates 200 twenty-round games and reports the average, so luck is filtered out.

The lesson

  • Reducing variability recovers almost all the lost throughput — for free, with less inventory. This is the statistical heart of lean/Six Sigma and of steady takt times.
  • Buffers (inventory, time, or capacity) also protect throughput — but you pay for them in work-in-process and lead time. Use them deliberately, at the constraint (Theory of Constraints: drum–buffer–rope).
  • Adding capacity works but is usually the most expensive lever — and a balanced line at higher capacity still loses to variability.
  • This is also why utilization near 100% explodes lead times: no slack to absorb fluctuations (queueing theory says congestion ∝ variability × utilization).

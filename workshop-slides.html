The rules — that's all of them

Each week: receive → ship → place ONE order

💰 Costs

  • Holding stock: $0.50/case/week
  • Unfilled orders (backlog): $1.00/case/week — and must still be shipped later

⏱️ Delays

  • Your order takes 1 week to reach your supplier
  • Shipments take 2 weeks to arrive

🤫 You see only your own inventory and incoming orders. No talking between groups. No showing screens.

What you just built

The bullwhip effect

Order variability amplifies at every step upstream.
A ripple at the shelf becomes a wave at the factory.

  • Named by Procter & Gamble: wild diaper orders at the factory — while babies consumed steadily
  • Typical amplification: 2× per tier, compounding
  • 2020–21: semiconductors, freight, farm equipment — the bullwhip at global scale
  • The bill: excess stock and stockouts, overtime then idle plants, expedited freight, forecasts nobody trusts

The uncomfortable truth

Nobody in this room was incompetent

Sterman ran this game thousands of times at MIT — executives, students, supply chain professors. Almost every chain produces the bullwhip.

The structure did it: delays between action and effect · each tier seeing only its neighbor's orders · forecasts chasing forecasts · and under pressure, everyone forgetting what they already ordered and ordering again.

Blame the system, not the player — then fix the system.

This is not just beer

The dealer network version

In the gameIn our world
Ordering from your neighbor's ordersPlanning from network orders instead of retail sales
Big lumpy ordersPre-season / early-order programs concentrating a year into a window
Panic over-ordering in shortageOver-ordering hot models to win allocation — then canceling
3-week loop felt like foreverMulti-month factory lead times on wholegoods
Forgetting what was in transitOrdering again while the order board already covers it

What actually fixes it

Share truth. Shorten loops. Trust the pipeline.

  • Share retail data — actual settlements/POS visible up the chain, not just orders
  • Order steadily — smaller, regular orders beat quarter-end and program-window spikes
  • Count the order board as inventory — on-order stock IS stock; don't order twice
  • Allocate on retail history, not order size — kills the gaming incentive
  • Cut and stabilize lead times — the shorter the loop, the smaller the whip

In the game, disciplined ordering against identical demand costs ~40% less. That margin exists in real networks too.

Risk Pooling

Lesson 2 priced uncertainty at a single location: safety stock. This lesson's question is where the stock should live — because the same service costs dramatically different amounts of inventory depending on how you aggregate the risk.

After this lesson you can

  • Split any stock position into cycle stock and safety stock — and know which one pools
  • Apply the square-root law: pooling n independent demands cuts safety stock by √n
  • Identify when pooling fails (correlation) and what it trades away (response time)
  • Spot the four disguises of pooling: centralization, postponement, commonality, virtual pooling

1. Two kinds of stock

Any warehouse's inventory splits cleanly in two:

  • Cycle stock covers average demand between replenishments. It scales with the mean.
  • Safety stock covers the swings. For a service level with safety factor z (Lesson 2's critical ratio in another costume), it scales with the standard deviation: SS = z·σ.

This split matters because the two behave completely differently when you reorganize the network. Averages add up no matter where the stock sits. Swings partially cancel when you pool them — one region's bad week is another's slow week.

2. The square-root law

Take n locations with independent demands, each with standard deviation σ. Serve them separately and total safety stock is n·z·σ. Serve them from one pool and the combined demand has standard deviation σ·√n — not σ·n — so:

SSpooled = z·σ·√n   vs.   SSseparate = z·σ·n   ⇒   pooling saves a factor of √n

Worked example (the game). Four regions, each Normal(100, 30) per week, 95% service (z = 1.645).

  • Four warehouses: each needs 100 + 1.645×30 ≈ 149 → total ≈ 597
  • One warehouse: demand is Normal(400, 60) — note σ = 30×√4 = 60, not 120 — so it needs 400 + 1.645×60 ≈ 499
  • Same 95% service, ~16% less total inventory — and the safety component fell by half (√4 = 2)

The savings compound with scale: 16 stores pooled = 4× less safety stock. This is why e-commerce, run from few large DCs, can carry enormous assortments that no individual store could justify — the "long tail" is a risk-pooling artifact.

3. When pooling fails, and what it costs

Correlation is the kill switch. The √n math assumes independence. If all regions boom and bust together — national promotions, weather, fashion, recessions — the swings don't cancel. At correlation ρ, pooled σ = σ·√(n + n(n−1)ρ): at ρ = 1 pooling saves exactly nothing. Ask of any pooling proposal: do these demands actually move independently?

Distance is the price. One national DC is, on average, farther from customers than regional ones — you trade inventory for transport cost and response time. That is a genuine strategic choice: Amazon began fully centralized (pooling maximal), then deliberately re-decentralized to buy same-day speed, paying for it with inventory. Neither answer is "right"; the trade-off is the point.

4. Pooling wears four disguises

  • Physical centralization — fewer, bigger warehouses (this lesson's game).
  • Postponement — hold generic product; differentiate at the last moment. Benetton famously dyed after knitting, pooling color risk across the season. HP localized printers in the regional DC, pooling country demand.
  • Component commonality — shared parts across products pool the demand risk of every product that uses them (one battery platform across car models).
  • Virtual pooling — keep stock where it is but share visibility and ship from anywhere: store-to-store transfers, ship-from-store, inventory-sharing agreements.

Same math every time: aggregate the uncertainty, shrink the buffer.

Now play — 10–15 minutes

🏭 The Warehouse Consolidation Challenge

Find the leanest stock that hits 95% fill with four regional warehouses, then with one central warehouse. Try to beat the theory numbers above (597 and 499). At the debrief, drag the correlation slider and watch the pooling benefit die as ρ → 1.

After the game — discuss or reflect

  • Compare your two locked-in totals against 597 vs. 499. Which stock component shrank — cycle or safety?
  • Name a demand in your business that's genuinely independent across regions, and one that's strongly correlated. What does that imply for each?
  • Your CFO wants one national DC ("16% less inventory!"). Your COO wants regional speed. Frame the debate in this lesson's terms.
  • Which of the four disguises could your company use without moving a single warehouse?

Self-check

1. Pooling 9 independent, identical demands into one location cuts total safety stock by…

√9 = 3. Nine separate buffers of z·σ become one buffer of z·σ·3 instead of z·σ·9. Cycle stock, by contrast, doesn't change at all.

2. Which situation gains the LEAST from consolidating regional warehouses?

Nationally synchronized spikes mean highly correlated regional demands — the swings don't cancel, so pooling's benefit collapses. The others are pooling's best customers.

3. Benetton dyeing sweaters after knitting them (instead of before) is an example of…

Undyed sweaters are a common component serving every color's demand — the color decision is postponed until uncertainty resolves. Classic risk pooling without touching the warehouse network.

Key takeaways

  • Cycle stock covers averages and never pools; safety stock covers swings and pools by √n.
  • Independence is the engine — correlation is the kill switch. Always ask how demands co-move.
  • Pooling trades inventory against response time; centralize vs. decentralize is a strategy choice, not arithmetic.
  • Postponement, commonality, and virtual pooling deliver the same math without moving buildings.

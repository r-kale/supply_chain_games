Incentives & Contracts

Lesson 5 showed a chain failing on information. This lesson shows a chain failing on incentives — even with perfect information. A supply chain is not one firm: each link optimizes its own profit, and the sum of local optima is reliably worse than the whole. The fix isn't trust or goodwill. It's contract design.

After this lesson you can

  • Explain double marginalization and why no wholesale price alone can fix it
  • Show how a buyback (or revenue-sharing) contract coordinates a chain
  • Separate growing the pie (coordination) from splitting it (negotiation)
  • Recognize coordinating contracts in the wild: returns, markdown money, VMI, revenue shares

1. Double marginalization: two margins, one broken chain

A manufacturer (cost c = $3) sells through a retailer (price p = $12) facing newsvendor demand ~ Normal(100, 30). Put Lesson 2's critical ratio to work twice:

  • If one company owned the whole chain, it would stock with CR = (12 − 3)/12 = 0.75 → Q ≈ 120 → expected profit ≈ $786 per season. That's the ceiling — the biggest possible pie.
  • Split into two firms with a wholesale price w = $9, the retailer's own margin is only 12 − 9 = $3 against $9 at risk per leftover: CR = 3/12 = 0.25 → the retailer orders ≈ 80, not 120.

The retailer is not being timid — it's optimizing correctly against its own margins. But 40 units of profitable sales never happen, and roughly $150 of expected profit per season simply evaporates. That's double marginalization (Spengler, 1950): two successive margins each shrink the decision below the chain optimum. And here's the trap: lower w and the pie grows but your slice thins; raise w and your margin fattens while the pie shrinks. No wholesale price gets both. You'll feel this personally in the game.

2. Contracts that coordinate

The escape is to change the retailer's risk, not just its margin. A buyback contract refunds b per unsold unit. Now the retailer's overage cost is w − b instead of w, so its critical ratio is (p − w)/(p − b) — and you can choose the (w, b) pair to make that equal the chain's 0.75:

coordinate when  (p − w)/(p − b) = (p − c)/p  ⇒  with p=12, c=3:  b = 12 − (12 − w)/0.75

At w = $9, b = $8: the retailer rationally orders 120 — the full pie is baked — while the manufacturer keeps most of it. Slide w up or down the coordination line and only the split changes, never the size. Coordination and negotiation become separate problems, which is precisely what makes the deal sellable to both sides: it's not zero-sum anymore.

Revenue sharing is the same medicine in a different bottle: charge a low wholesale price (low risk to the retailer) in exchange for a share of revenue. Cachon & Lariviere proved the two are financially equivalent. Other family members: quantity flexibility (supplier absorbs forecast error), options contracts (buy capacity rights now, exercise later), markdown money (brand funds clearance discounts).

3. The Blockbuster story

1997: a VHS tape cost stores ~$65; rentals earned ~$3. That's a brutal newsvendor problem — CR tiny, so stores stocked few copies, and 20% of Friday customers found the shelf empty for the hit they wanted. 1998: Blockbuster and the studios switched to revenue sharing — tapes at ~$8 up front plus ~half of each rental. Store risk collapsed, stocking exploded, availability jumped, rentals rose ~75%, and both sides earned more. Nobody got more generous; the contract changed what was individually rational.

4. Beyond price: aligning the whole relationship

Contract terms are one instrument in a bigger orchestra. Vendor-managed inventory hands the stocking decision to whoever has the best information and incentives. Cost-plus transparency and gain-sharing split efficiency wins. And note the closing symmetry of this course: Lesson 5's chain failed because information didn't flow; this one fails because incentives don't align. Real supply chain excellence — the Toyotas, the Zaras — is having engineered both.

Now play — 10–15 minutes

🤝 The Contract Game

You're the manufacturer; a rational retailer responds to whatever terms you offer. Spend a few seasons trying to win with wholesale price alone — feel the pie shrink as you squeeze. Then find the buyback terms where the retailer orders 120 and you still keep most of the profit. Teaching a group? Let them discover the buyback field without hints.

After the game — discuss or reflect

  • What was your best season's chain percentage before you touched buyback? After?
  • "Refund leftovers" sounds like charity. Using the numbers, explain why it's greed done properly.
  • Your procurement team is measured purely on purchase price variance. What does this lesson predict they'll do to total chain profit?
  • Which of your supplier or customer relationships has a shrunken pie right now? What risk-shifting term would grow it?

Self-check

1. Double marginalization causes the retailer to order less than the chain optimum because…

Perfectly rational, perfectly informed — but optimizing against margin (p − w) instead of (p − c). The gap between those margins is the profit that evaporates.

2. A buyback contract coordinates the chain by…

The refund shrinks Co from w to w − b, raising the retailer's CR to the integrated chain's level — so the privately optimal order IS the chain optimal order.

3. On the coordination line, raising the wholesale price w (with b adjusted to stay on the line) changes…

That separation is the whole point: coordination (pie size) and negotiation (pie split) become independent. Bargain as hard as you like about w — the chain stays whole.

Key takeaways

  • Independent margins shrink the pie: local optimization ≠ chain optimization, even with perfect information and perfect rationality.
  • No wholesale price alone can coordinate. Risk-shifting terms — buybacks, revenue shares, flexibility, options — can.
  • Grow the pie first, then negotiate the split. Coordinating contracts make the negotiation non-zero-sum.
  • Information (L5) and incentives (L6) are the two great chain-breakers. Engineer both.

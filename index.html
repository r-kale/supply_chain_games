📦 Supply Chain Games

Learn supply chain concepts by playing them

Reading about the bullwhip effect is one thing — causing it yourself is another. These short browser games put you inside classic supply chain dilemmas, then debrief you with your own data. No installs, no accounts. Play solo or run them as team workshops.

Bullwhip effect

🍺 The Beer Game

Run one tier of a four-stage beer supply chain. Watch a tiny change in customer demand turn into wild swings of over-ordering and stockouts upstream — the famous bullwhip effect.

Solo vs. bots, or hot-seat with 2–4 players · 20–40 min

 Demand uncertainty

📰 The Newsvendor Game

Order stock for a product that expires at the end of each day. Order too much and you eat the leftovers; too little and you lose sales. Find the profit-maximizing balance — and discover the bias almost everyone shares.

Solo · 10–15 min

 Risk pooling

🏭 The Warehouse Consolidation Challenge

Stock four regional warehouses, then one central warehouse serving the same customers. Hit the same service level with far less inventory — and see when pooling stops working.

Solo · 10–15 min

 Variability & flow

🎲 The Dice Game

A five-station production line where every station's capacity is a die roll. Average capacity says 3.5 units a round — so why does the line never deliver it? The classic exercise from The Goal.

Solo · 10 min

 Incentives & contracts

🤝 The Contract Game

You're the manufacturer; a rational retailer orders whatever your terms make worth ordering. Squeeze the price and the pie shrinks. Discover the contract trick that grows it back — refunding leftovers.

Solo · 10–15 min

 Batching & EOQ

⚖️ The Batch Size Game

Big batches waste warehouse space; small batches drown you in ordering costs. Hunt down the sweet spot, then learn why lean factories attack the fixed cost instead of tuning the batch.

Solo · 5–10 min

Running these with a team

Each game ends with a debrief screen built from the session's own data — charts, costs, and the theory behind what just happened. Every debrief also issues a result code: collect your teams' codes and paste them into the 🏆 Comparison Board for instant leaderboards — no accounts, no server. For workshops:

  • Beer Game: use hot-seat mode with up to four players on one machine and a projector for the debrief, or have several solo groups race on total cost. Don't reveal the customer demand pattern beforehand — the reveal is the lesson.
  • Newsvendor: have everyone play both scenarios individually, then compare average orders. The pull-to-center bias shows up in almost every group.
  • Risk pooling & Dice Game: great as 15-minute openers before discussing network design or lean/flow topics.

A full facilitator guide with timings, discussion questions, and the theory for each game is in FACILITATOR_GUIDE.md.

The concepts, in one line each

GameConceptThe one-line takeaway
Beer GameBullwhip effectOrder variability amplifies upstream; share demand data and trust your supply line.
NewsvendorService level / critical ratioThe right order depends on the cost of too much vs. too little, not on average demand.
ConsolidationRisk poolingAggregating independent demands cuts safety stock roughly by √n.
Dice GameVariability + dependencyIn a connected line, you lose to variability — averages overpromise what flow delivers.
Contract GameDouble marginalizationIndependent margins shrink the pie; coordinating contracts (buybacks, revenue sharing) grow it back.
Batch SizeEOQ / batchingBalance ordering vs. holding cost — then shrink the fixed cost itself, like lean does.

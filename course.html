📦 Supply Chain Games / Course

A complete short course · 6 lessons · ~6–8 hours total

Supply Chain Fundamentals, learned by playing

Six lessons, each paired with a game that makes you feel the concept before the theory names it. Read the lesson, play the game, work the discussion questions, pass the self-check. By the end you'll understand why supply chains fail — and the small set of ideas that fix them.

How to use this course

  • Self-study: one lesson per sitting (60–75 min including the game). Play honestly — the games are calibrated so your mistakes are the curriculum.
  • Instructor-led: for maximum effect, run the game first, then teach the lesson as the explanation of what everyone just experienced (especially Lessons 5 and 6 — don't spoil the trap). The facilitator guide has timings and debrief scripts; the Comparison Board turns any lesson into a tournament.
  • The sequence matters: each lesson leans on the previous ones — variability, then one decision, then a network, then a policy, then whole-chain dynamics, then incentives.

Why this order

The course climbs a ladder of scope. Lesson 1 starts inside a single process: variability is the enemy, and flow obeys laws. Lesson 2 narrows to a single decision under that uncertainty — the atomic unit of inventory thinking. Lesson 3 widens to a network: where should buffers live? Lesson 4 adds the policy dimension: how often and how much? With all four in hand, Lesson 5 releases them into a multi-company chain with delays and local information — and the bullwhip emerges from exactly the ingredients you've studied. Lesson 6 closes with the piece no operations math fixes: firms optimizing locally against each other, and the contracts that realign them.

Alternative openings: many instructors run the Beer Game cold on day one as a hook — nothing sells the course like causing a disaster personally. That works fine here: play it from the multiplayer page first, skip the debrief discussion, and return to Lesson 5 later with data already on the table.

Assessment ideas

  • Self-checks: every lesson ends with a three-question quiz with explanations.
  • Game benchmarks: beat the disciplined-bot cost in the Beer Game; land within 2% in the Batch Size Game; reach 100% coordination in the Contract Game; beat theory (597/499) in Consolidation.
  • Tournament: collect result codes from every participant into the Comparison Board and debrief the spread — the variance between teams is itself a lesson in how much decisions matter.
  • Capstone exercise: have each participant name one real process, its bullwhip sources, and the single countermeasure with the best effort-to-impact ratio.

Inventory under Uncertainty

Lesson 1 showed variability wrecking flow inside a process. Now point it at demand. You must commit to a quantity before you know what customers will want — the single most common decision in supply chains. There is a right answer, and it's probably not the one your gut gives.

After this lesson you can

  • Frame any one-shot stocking decision as a trade-off between overage and underage costs
  • Compute the critical ratio and read the optimal quantity off the demand distribution
  • Explain why the optimal order is almost never average demand
  • Recognize pull-to-center bias — in others and in yourself

1. The newsvendor problem

A newspaper seller stocks papers at dawn; unsold copies at dusk are worthless. Fashion buys, holiday inventory, fresh food, concert seats, flu vaccine orders, airline overbooking — structurally identical: one decision, then demand happens.

Every unit you stock is a small bet with asymmetric stakes:

  • Cu — underage cost: what one unit of unmet demand costs you (usually the lost margin, sometimes also goodwill).
  • Co — overage cost: what one leftover unit costs you (cost minus any salvage value).

2. The critical ratio

Think marginally. The Q-th unit you stock sells only if demand reaches Q. Stock it if the expected win beats the expected loss: P(D ≥ Q) × Cu > P(D < Q) × Co. Keep adding units until that stops being true, and you get the newsvendor rule:

Choose Q* so that  P(demand ≤ Q*) = Cu / (Cu + Co)  — the critical ratio (CR)

The critical ratio is a service level: the probability you don't stock out. Notice what's in the formula — two costs. And what isn't — average demand. The average only tells you where the distribution sits; the economics tell you how far up it to stock.

Worked example (the game you'll play). Croissants sell at $12, cost $3, leftovers are tossed. Demand is roughly Normal(100, 30).

  • Cu = 12 − 3 = $9 (margin lost per turned-away customer); Co = $3 (cost of a tossed croissant)
  • CR = 9 / (9 + 3) = 0.75 → stock at the 75th percentile of demand
  • 75th percentile of Normal(100, 30) ≈ 100 + 0.674 × 30 ≈ 120 croissants — a fifth above average

Now flip the margins: cost $9, price $12. CR = 3/12 = 0.25 → stock the 25th percentile ≈ 80 — a fifth below average. Same customers, same demand, opposite answer. Margins, not means, set the order.

3. The bias everyone brings

Schweitzer & Cachon (2000) ran exactly this experiment on hundreds of subjects — including experienced managers. The result replicates everywhere: people order too little when margins are high and too much when margins are low. Orders get dragged toward average demand, as if the mean were a magnet. It's called pull-to-center, and it has two roots: anchoring on the average, and the sting of visible waste (leftovers hurt more than invisible lost sales).

In real assortments this shows up as a systematic pattern: star products chronically understocked, marginal products chronically overstocked — precisely backwards from what the economics say.

4. Where you'll meet this again

Every "service level" in an inventory system — "we target 95% availability" — is a critical ratio wearing a suit. Choosing 95% is choosing a ratio of underage to overage cost of 19:1, whether anyone did that math or not. Lesson 3 builds directly on this: once you know the service level you want, safety stock is what it costs — and network design changes the price.

Now play — 10–15 minutes

📰 The Newsvendor Game

Play both scenarios for real — don't compute first, order by feel, and don't change your approach mid-scenario. The debrief will plot your average against the optimum. Teaching a group? Have everyone play before anyone discusses, then poll the room's average orders — the shared bias is the lesson.

After the game — discuss or reflect

  • Where did your averages land relative to 120 and 80? Toward the middle? You've met pull-to-center.
  • Whose fault is a stockout of a high-margin item: the forecaster or the order policy?
  • Your company targets the same service level for every product. Given CR = economics, what's wrong with that?
  • Which decisions in your business are secretly newsvendor problems (capacity bookings, staffing levels, event stock)?

Self-check

1. A grocer buys strawberries at $4/box, sells at $10/box; unsold boxes are donated (worth $0 to the grocer). The critical ratio is…

Cu = 10 − 4 = 6, Co = 4. CR = 6/(6+4) = 0.6 → stock the 60th percentile of demand, a bit above the mean.

2. Demand averages 200/day. The optimal order is 260. What must be true?

Ordering above the mean means the critical ratio exceeds 0.5 — losing a sale hurts more than a leftover. The forecast can be perfectly correct.

3. Pull-to-center bias predicts that a merchandiser managing both a high-margin hit and a low-margin filler product will tend to…

Orders drift toward average demand from both directions: the high-CR product gets less than optimal, the low-CR product more. The profitable item stocks out while the marginal one piles up.

Key takeaways

  • One-shot stocking = a bet per unit: Cu against Co.
  • Order at the critical ratio percentile: CR = Cu/(Cu+Co). Average demand is where you start counting, not the answer.
  • High margin → stock above the mean; low margin → below. Every service-level target is a critical ratio in disguise.
  • Humans pull orders toward the mean. Knowing the bias is the first defense; computing CR is the second.

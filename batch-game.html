⚖️ The Batch Size Game

You manage one warehouse item that sells a steady 100 units every week. Every order you place costs $400 in fixed charges (freight, receiving, paperwork) no matter the size — but every unit sitting in the warehouse costs $0.30 per week to hold. Order rarely in big batches, or often in small ones?

The Economic Order Quantity (1913 — and still everywhere)

EOQ = √( 2 × K × D / h ) = √( 2 × 400 × 100 / 0.30 ) ≈ 516 units

where K = fixed cost per order, D = demand rate, h = holding cost. At the optimum, ordering and holding costs are exactly balanced — that's why the curve bottoms there.

Two insights worth keeping

  • The curve is forgiving. Ordering 20% off the EOQ costs only ~2% extra. Don't chase precision in Q — chase the inputs (is K real? is h honest about capital, shrinkage, obsolescence?).
  • The powerful move is shrinking K, not tuning Q. Halve the fixed order cost and the optimal batch drops by √2 — smaller batches, less inventory, faster flow. This is the math behind lean's obsession with setup-time reduction (SMED): Toyota didn't solve EOQ, they attacked K until batches of one made sense.
  • Batching feeds the bullwhip. Big, lumpy orders are one of the four classic bullwhip causes you saw in the Beer Game — another reason small K matters.

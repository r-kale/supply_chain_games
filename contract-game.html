🤝 The Contract Game

You manufacture a seasonal product for $3 a unit. A retailer sells it for $12 to customers whose seasonal demand averages 100 (±30). Each season you offer the retailer contract terms; the retailer then decides how much to order. Whatever they don't sell is wasted. You'll play 8 seasons — maximize your total profit.

How the retailer thinks

  • The retailer is sharp: given your terms, it orders exactly the quantity that maximizes its own expected profit (and orders nothing if your terms would lose it money).
  • You choose the wholesale price — and optionally a buyback price: how much you'll refund per unsold unit at season's end. Refunding leftovers sounds crazy. Maybe it isn't.
  • Scores use expected (long-run average) profits, so there's no luck — only strategy.

Why a plain wholesale price can't capture the full pie

Total chain profit vs. wholesale price with no buyback. Raising your price makes the retailer order less — the chain shrinks as you squeeze.

Double marginalization — two margins, one broken chain

The retailer orders using its margin (retail − wholesale), not the chain's margin (retail − production cost). With a wholesale price above your cost, the retailer's margin is thinner than the chain's, so it rationally orders less than the chain-optimal quantity. Every dollar of wholesale margin you grab shrinks the total pie you're grabbing from.

Buyback contracts coordinate the chain

Refunding leftovers lowers the retailer's overstock risk, so it orders more. Choose the pair (wholesale w, buyback b) on the coordination line and the retailer orders exactly the chain-optimal 120 units — the pie is maximized — while w slides your share up or down:

coordinate when  (12 − w) / (12 − b) = (12 − 3) / 12 = 0.75  →  b = 12 − (12 − w)/0.75

e.g. w = $9, b = $8: retailer still orders 120, and you keep most of the profit.

Where you've seen this

  • Blockbuster & the studios (1998): revenue-sharing (a buyback cousin) let stores stock 10× more tapes — rentals up ~75%, both sides won.
  • Publishing: unsold book returns are a standing buyback contract.
  • Fashion "markdown money", vendor rebates, quantity flexibility clauses — all attempts to fix the same broken incentive.
